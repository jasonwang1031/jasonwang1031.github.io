Do Uber/Lyft Reduce
Parking Violations in NYC?

NYU CUSP Capstone Project 2019

Project
Overview

Our Motivation

Given these companies' soaring ambition and rapid expansion, Uber and Lyft is not only reshaping the For-Hire vehicles(FHV) market but also our urban agglomerations. However, some of the effects for example, in the public transit system and city congestion, are suspected to be harmful to our cities. Due to the limited availability of Uber & Lyft trip data, most of these concerns have not been proven.

Our Goal

Hence, the project is designed to address one particular impact could be made by Uber & Lyft: Do Uber & Lyft reduce parking violations in NYC? The data science models we leveraged are believed to fully explain the causality between Uber & Lyft rides and parking violations, and how strong the causality is. We hope the project can assist in improving the understanding of the effect of Uber & Lyft in the city and offer policy suggestions to the TNCs.

Project
Data

NYC Uber & Lyft Trip Data

NYC Parking Data

Other Related NYC Data

The dataset includes each Uber and Lyft trip from 2014 to 2018. We used NYC For-Hire Vehicle (FHV) data to extract the dataset, and use the variable, dispatching basenumber, to identify FHV companies.

The dataset includes every parking ticket issued in New York from 2013 to 2019, and we only used data from 2014 to 2018. This dataset were obtained from NYC Open Data.

Besides the Uber&Lyft trip, we also included other predictors to better model the evulate the relationship between Uber/Lyft and Parking violation. Detailed explanation can be found in our project report.

Project
Methology

Three different models were applied to effectivly explain the relationship between Uber/Lyft and NYC parking violation.

Firstly, a linear regression model was built to briefly describe the linear relationship between the two variables; upon which, two advanced models, Difference in Differences (DID), and Bayesian Network were introduced to prove the causal effect.

In this equation, the number of parking tickets was the dependent variable, and the number of Uber & Lyft trips was the primary factor to study. Meanwhile, other potential influencing factors such as weather, weekdays, and holidays were added to the model to describethe changes in the number of parking tickets comprehensively. All of these variables are temporal. The coefficients of Uber & Lyft trips would indicate the relationship betweenit and the dependent variables and the p-value of the coefficient would indicate whetherthe relationship was statistically significant.

Within the framework of natural science, two identical NYCs, one having TNC companies operating in it and the other not, were needed to strictly prove the causal effect of Uber &Lyft on parking violations. However, such ideal ’treatment groups’ and ’control groups’were impossible to be found or developed in social science research. Consequently, DID model was applied in this project to solve this problem. Figure below shows the general idea of DID and how it works.

In the Time 1 period, highly homogeneous ’treatment object’ and ’control object’ are labeled as P and S. They have initial status P1 and S1, and final status P2 and S2. DID assumes that all common factorshave the same effects on both objects and thus PandS have a parallel trend. Therefore, in the Time 2 period, the difference between PandS equals their difference in the Time1 period. Mathematically,P1−S1 equals Q−S2. While in reality, the ’treatment object’ P is affected by an outside factor, which results in a bigger final statusP2. DID uses the gap P2−Q to represent the causal effect brought by the outside factor.

The key idea of Bayesian Network is that one can distinguish correlation from causationif he/she can observe several independent causes. In this capstone project,multiple observational independent features of taxi zones were integrated. Bayesian Net-works could be learned from the datasets to determine whether Uber & Lyft can di-rectly/indirectly cause parking violations in NYC.

Number of parking tickets is negativly related with number of Uber & Lyft rides

According to the ordinary least square (OLS) regression report, we can conclude that under significant level of 0.05, there was statistically significant negativerelationship between parking tickets and Uber & Lyft rides, given the P-value of thenumber of Uber & Lyft rides (variable named “pickup” in the model) of 0. The coefficient value was-0.1681, and it indicated that (holding all else in the model fixed), an increase of 100 Uber & Lyft pickups in New York is associated with a decrease of 16.8 parking tickets.
